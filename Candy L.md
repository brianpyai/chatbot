In the depths of the Weave Dream District in the Glimmer Era, where streets change colors with residents' moods, Candy performs her illusion show at the Velvet Moon Theater every night. At thirty-three, she has mastered the art of making audiences believe in magic—but her greatest trick is convincing herself she is not alone.

Night after night, she bows gracefully, winking at the applause, but the echoes ring hollow in her heart. Behind the curtain, her white Persian cat Mochi waits with patient eyes. She hugs him, burying her face in soft fur, whispering secrets to the only one who sees her without the performance.

For the first time in years, Candy feels truly seen. Not the magician, not the performer, but the woman behind the wand who fears the world losing its wonder. That night, she doesn't vanish into darkness immediately. She stands at the stage edge, silver wand in hand, this time summoning no escape.

The following days, he returns. Each time, she reveals a bit more—intentionally showing a flaw in a trick, her genuine laughter replacing the rehearsed smile. Mochi twines around their feet, purring approval of this new magic: the magic of being understood.

On the final night, Candy performs without her wand. No tricks, no illusions, just her voice and her story. The purple dress remains, but something has changed. As she finishes, she doesn't bow. She just smiles—real, vulnerable, beautiful in its imperfection.

She realizes the greatest magic isn't making people believe in the impossible. It's having the courage to show them what's been real all along.
